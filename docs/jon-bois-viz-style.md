# Jon Bois Visual Storytelling Research

## Signature Visual Techniques

**Google Earth as Canvas**: The core technique. Bois places charts, newspaper clippings, photos, and 3D models INTO Google Earth, then flies a virtual camera through them. Data becomes physical landscape you navigate. This is the single most defining technique.

**Iterative Collage Building**: Visuals accumulate over runtime. Each chart, clipping, or image stays in the 3D space as new ones are added, building a "game board" where no data point stands alone. The camera pulls back to reveal the full landscape of evidence.

**Google Sheets Charts**: Charts are made in Google Sheets -- deliberately simple, not polished D3 or Tableau. Scatter plots, line charts, bar charts with default-ish styling. The simplicity is the point.

## Chart/Visualization Types

- **Scatter plots** (his signature -- plotting every NFL score, every punt, etc.)
- **Timelines** (careers, seasons, historical arcs)
- **Line charts** (performance over time)
- **Bar charts** (comparative statistics)
- **Maps** (Google Earth geographical context)
- **Newspaper clipping collages** (historical evidence layered in space)

## Narrative Data Presentation

Bois finds statistical outliers and absurdities, then builds emotional narratives around them. He asks "what does this data FEEL like?" not just "what does it show?" He makes the insignificant significant -- overlooked players, bizarre records, forgotten moments become profound through data context.

## Major Series

- **Pretty Good** (2015+): True stories of unusual events, standalone episodes
- **Chart Party** (2016+): Statistical deep dives into sports oddities (every NFL score, athletes named Bob)
- **Dorktown**: Co-hosted data documentary series, 40-60 min episodes
- **The History of the Seattle Mariners** (2020): 6-part, multi-hour magnum opus
- **The History of the Atlanta Falcons** (2021)
- **The History of the Minnesota Vikings** (2024)

## What Makes It Distinctive

1. **Anti-production-value aesthetic**: No slick motion graphics. Google Sheets + Google Earth. Intentionally "computer-y"
2. **Vaporwave-adjacent horizons**: Digital landscapes with glitchy, unsettled rendering
3. **Smooth jazz soundtrack**: Keith Mansfield, Alan Hawkshaw -- creates meditative contrast with data
4. **Emotional data**: Charts become characters; the camera lingers on a data point the way a film lingers on a face
5. **Universal appeal**: Makes non-sports-fans care by finding human stories in numbers
6. **Accumulative context**: Nothing stands alone; every visualization exists alongside all previous ones in shared 3D space

## Animation and Pacing

- Camera swoops, tilts, and dives through 3D data landscapes like an aircraft
- Slow reveals -- zooming out to show the full picture after building piece by piece
- Sudden appearances of visual elements whose meaning clicks from pre-established context
- Measured, unhurried pacing that lets data breathe

## Tools and Aesthetic Choices

- **Google Earth Studio**: Primary animation/camera tool
- **Google Sheets**: Chart creation (deliberately simple)
- **Multicolored graphs**: Against digital landscape backgrounds
- **Vaporwave-style horizons**: Pixel-built frozen imagery
- **Minimal typography**: Clean, unadorned text overlays
- **Smooth jazz**: Musical signature creating contemplative mood
- **No talking heads, no B-roll**: Pure data + narration

## Design System Implications

To replicate the Jon Bois spirit in a visualization platform:

1. **Scatter plots as primary viz** -- find the outliers, highlight the absurd
2. **Accumulative dashboards** -- charts that build on each other, not isolated widgets
3. **Fly-through navigation** -- spatial relationship between data views
4. **Simple chart styling** -- resist over-polishing; Google Sheets energy
5. **Narrative sequencing** -- guided tours through data, not static dashboards
6. **Outlier detection** -- algorithmically surface the bizarre and beautiful
7. **Smooth transitions** -- camera-like movement between data views
8. **Musical/ambient layer** -- audio design matters for the experience
